Ai for peacebuilding

Facebook runs one of the forefront labs in machine learning (AI), headed by recent Turing award winner Yan LeCun and backed by one of the largest datacenters ever built. These resources present an opportunity to think about how AI can play a role in the digital future of peacebuilding. In the space of AI for peacebuilding on social media platforms, there seem to be two central AI challenges:

  1. Auto-flag harmful and borderline content
  2. Predict which posts will bring people together

Which posts will bring people together? Most of the field of AI for peacebuilding has focused on the first problem (auto-flagging harmful content). However, the study of the history of peacebuilding shows the importance of reconciliation and exposure. This objective fits with Facebook's mission which is to "to bring people together." Facebook has already stated that its Newsfeed algorithm will "prioritize posts that spark conversations and meaningful interactions between people" [3]. A peacebuilding variation of this goal could be to prioritize posts which create a positive, conciliatory interaction between people of opposing ethnic/political affiliations. Rigorously defining this objective remains an open problem as does defining who counts as being part of a monitored ethnic/political affiliation. A naive definition might upweight posts that foster a common enemy, potentially simply redirecting othering. This is especially easy to imagine in an algorithm where the content of a post is not taken into consideration, only whether or not it was liked by people of different ethnic affiliations. Both tasks are hard. They have similar, daunting technical obstacles as both require nuanced understanding of the content of posts and messages. Specifically (1) It is famously hard for computer to understand human language (2) Posts in at-risk countries are often written in languages which modern AI algorithms have trouble understanding, such as Arabic or Swahili, or even in tribal languages such as Luo, Kikuyu, Rohingya which (a) don't have much online data from which machines can learn and (b) sometimes don't have written forms resulting in inconsistent transliteration into digital platforms (3) A lot of content is built into images and while optical character recognition is increasingly good, it is still challenging (4) Adversarial Networks will become ever more competent at modifying posts to trick automatic moderators. (5) And last, but not least, subtle understanding of cultural context is well beyond the capabilities of any contemporary artificial intelligence algorithm. What counts as re-appropriated language?

In the Rwandan Genocide, the call to kill Hutis was broadcast by RTLM radio. We now understand that the calls for genocide were hidden in dog-whistles and placed alongside sophisticated use of humor and popular Zairean music [1]. Detecting the modern equivalent, perhaps a handwritten meme in Burmese on a social media platform, remains difficult.

There is reason to hope. Facebook, and other social media companies have already shown initial success at detecting harmful or borderline content using a combination of machine learning algorithms and human raters. The monotonic increase in data and algorithmic sophistication suggests that we will make progress on parts of the task. For the challenges which will may have small amounts of data (understanding shifting cultural context, minority languages) breakthroughs in "one-shot learning" are encouraging. If humans can acquire understanding of a subject after small amounts of training, perhaps AI will one day be able to as well. Along the way we may learn more about how extremists operate.
